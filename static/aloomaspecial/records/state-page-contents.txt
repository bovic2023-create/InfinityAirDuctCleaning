Duct Condition in Older #State Homes<h2>
Many residential properties in #State have ductwork installed during original construction that has never received professional cleaning. Over years of operation, the duct interior accumulates debris from the system itself: fibers from air filters that allowed fine particles through, skin cells and dander from occupants, biological material from condensation events inside the air handler, and construction debris from any renovation work done while the system was running.
This accumulated debris does not stay neatly in one place. Debris-coated horizontal duct runs increase the static pressure the blower must work against. Debris on the evaporator coil surface reduces coil efficiency. Debris in the blower wheel unbalances it, increases vibration, and accelerates bearing wear. The cumulative effect on system performance is real and measurable. We measure static pressure and blower wheel condition on every duct cleaning visit in #State and document the finding before and after the service.

Geothermal Heat Pump Installation Across #State<h2>
Geothermal heat pumps perform efficiently across every climate zone in #State, because their heat source and heat sink is the earth rather than the outdoor air. The ground temperature at six to ten feet of depth in #State holds between approximately 50 and 60 degrees Fahrenheit year-round regardless of whether it is August or February at the surface. A geothermal heat pump draws on that stable temperature to move heat into your #State home in winter and to reject heat from your home into the earth in summer.
For #State properties with sufficient land area, horizontal closed-loop systems bury polyethylene pipe loops in trenches typically six to eight feet deep and four to six feet wide. A rule of thumb for horizontal loop field sizing is approximately 400 to 600 linear feet of pipe per ton of system capacity, though actual requirements vary with soil thermal conductivity, which we measure during the site assessment. Sandy or dry soils in some parts of #State have lower thermal conductivity than clay-rich or moist soils and require longer loop fields to achieve equivalent heat exchange rates.
For #State properties with limited land area, vertical bore systems drill boreholes typically 150 to 400 feet deep and install U-bend loops at depth. Vertical systems cost more to install due to drilling cost but require a fraction of the surface land area of horizontal systems. They are the appropriate configuration for most #State urban and suburban properties with small lot sizes.
The indoor heat pump unit distributes conditioned air through your existing ductwork if the system is sized and configured correctly. We assess ductwork compatibility as part of the geothermal site assessment for every #State installation.

Preventive HVAC Maintenance Across #State<h2>
Annual preventive maintenance in #State addresses the specific component degradation patterns that #State's climate produces. In #State regions with high cooling load, run capacitors experience significant thermal stress through the long summer season. Capacitor degradation is progressive: a capacitor at 80 percent of rated microfarad value still runs the system while shortening the compressor's service life on every startup. Measuring the capacitor against its rated tolerance and replacing one that has degraded below acceptable range costs far less than the compressor failure it prevents.
Condensate drain lines in #State homes serving humid summer conditions accumulate biological growth that can block the drain and cause the system to shut down on a high-water safety cutout. Annual condensate drain flushing and treatment prevents this failure mode.
Contactor surfaces in the outdoor unit accumulate pitting and carbon tracking from normal arc-on and arc-off cycling. A heavily pitted contactor draws poorly and can fail to close consistently, causing intermittent no-cooling symptoms. Annual contactor inspection catches this before it becomes an unexplained service call.

Air Conditioner Repair in #State<h2>
When an air conditioner fails in #State summer conditions, the diagnostic visit begins with refrigerant circuit measurements, not assumptions. High-side and low-side gauge readings compared against the refrigerant manufacturer's pressure-temperature chart for the specific refrigerant in the system tell a technician whether the system has a charge issue, a compressor issue, or a restriction. Superheat and subcooling measurements refine the picture.
If the diagnosis finds low refrigerant, the correct response is to locate and repair the leak before adding refrigerant. Refrigerant does not deplete through normal operation: a system that is low on refrigerant has a leak somewhere in the sealed circuit. Adding refrigerant without finding and fixing the leak produces a temporary result followed by the same failure within weeks or months.
We perform leak detection using electronic refrigerant detectors calibrated to the parts-per-million sensitivity needed to locate slow leaks in coils and fittings, and UV dye injection for leaks that electronic detection does not locate precisely. We repair the leak, verify the repair, and recharge the system to the manufacturer's specified superheat and subcooling targets.
